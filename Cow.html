🐄 Cow Rescue Guide

🚑 Injured Cow Rescue ▶

⚠ Cows weigh 1,000+ lbs - Never put yourself in dangerous positions!

First aid for injured cows:

  1. Assess from a safe distance - look for lameness, bleeding, or labored breathing
  2. Approach from the side (never directly front/rear) speaking calmly
  3. For bleeding: use clean towels and steady pressure
  4. Check for bloat (left side distension) - emergency condition
  5. Move only if absolutely necessary - call professionals for transport
  6. Provide fresh water but withhold food if internal injury suspected

🔥 Heat Stress Emergency ▶

Signs of heat stress (can be fatal):

  1. Excessive panting (80+ breaths/minute)
  2. Salivation, stumbling, or collapse
  3. Move to shade immediately - use sprinklers to wet skin (not just hair)
  4. Offer cool (not cold) electrolyte water
  5. Use fans if available - cows need air movement
🚗 Cow on Roadway ▶

Securing loose cattle:

  1. Alert authorities immediately - cows can cause deadly accidents
  2. Create visual barriers with vehicles (park diagonally with hazards on)
  3. Use feed (hay, grain) to lure - never chase or yell
  4. Look for ear tags or brands to identify owner
  5. Keep at least 50 feet from bulls or protective mothers
🕳 Downed Cow Rescue ▶

Helping a cow that can't rise:

  1. Determine if alert (will respond to stimuli) or non-responsive
  2. Check for obvious injuries before attempting to move
  3. Provide cushioning (straw bales) if on hard surface
  4. Roll every 2-4 hours to prevent compartment syndrome
  5. Professional equipment (hip lifters) required for standing attempts
🤰 Calving Emergency ▶

When to intervene in birth:

  1. Normal labor lasts 2-4 hours - intervene if no progress after 1 hour of straining
  2. Clean hands/arms thoroughly before any internal examination
  3. Only pull when cow pushes - never use vehicles/tractors
  4. Call vet immediately if malpresentation (legs/head not positioned correctly)
  5. After birth, ensure calf breathes and cow passes placenta within 12 hours
