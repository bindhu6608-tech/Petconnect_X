🐐 Goat Rescue Guide

🚑 Injured Goat Rescue ▶

⚠ Goats may headbutt when scared - approach from the side!

First aid for injured goats:

  1. Approach calmly while speaking softly - avoid direct eye contact
  2. Use a towel to gently restrain the head before handling
  3. Check for wounds, limping, or labored breathing
  4. For bleeding: apply pressure with clean gauze
  5. Check for bloat (swollen left side) - emergency condition
  6. Transport in a well-ventilated carrier with non-slip flooring

🌿 Poisoning or Toxic Plant Ingestion ▶

Signs of poisoning:

  1. Drooling, diarrhea, or sudden collapse
  2. Identify the plant if possible (take photos)
  3. Call poison control immediately - do NOT induce vomiting
  4. Provide activated charcoal if advised by vet
  5. Keep goat warm and quiet while awaiting help
🏞 Stuck or Entangled Goat ▶

Rescuing a trapped goat:

  1. Assess the situation safely - goats often get heads/horns stuck
  2. Use wire cutters for fencing (protect yourself from kicks)
  3. For high places: place straw bales below as cushion
  4. Check for injuries after freeing - goats may hide pain
  5. Provide water and monitor for shock
🤰 Kidding (Birthing) Emergency ▶

When to intervene in birth:

  1. Normal labor lasts 30 min-2 hours per kid
  2. Intervene if: no progress after 30 min of active pushing
  3. Clean hands thoroughly before any assistance
  4. Only pull when doe pushes - never force
  5. Call vet immediately for malpresentation (wrong positioning)
🐐 Feral or Abandoned Baby Goat ▶

Caring for orphaned kids:

  1. Check if mother is nearby (don't assume abandonment)
  2. Keep warm with heating pad (on low, under towels)
  3. Feed goat milk replacer (never cow milk) every 4 hours
  4. Stimulate to urinate/defecate with warm damp cloth
  5. Contact goat rescue for long-term care
