🐔 Hen Rescue Guide

🚑 Injured Hen Rescue ▶

⚠ Hens hide injuries well - check for limping, drooping wings, or abnormal posture!

First aid for injured hens:

  1. Approach calmly from the side (never from above like a predator)
  2. Gently wrap in a towel, covering the eyes to reduce stress
  3. Check for wounds, mites/lice, or breathing difficulties
  4. For bleeding: apply cornstarch or styptic powder (never human bandages)
  5. Separate from flock immediately (other hens may peck injuries)
  6. Transport in a well-ventilated box with non-slip flooring

🌡 Heat Stress or Hypothermia ▶

Signs of temperature distress:

  1. Heat stress: Panting, wings held away from body, pale comb
  2. Hypothermia: Shivering, puffed feathers, lethargy
  3. For overheating: offer electrolytes in water, mist legs (not body)
  4. For cold: warm gradually with heating pad under half the enclosure
  5. Always provide fresh water - dehydrated hens won't eat
🏠 Abandoned or Escaped Domestic Hen ▶

Rescuing domestic hens:

  1. Lure with scratch grains or mealworms (more effective than bread)
  2. Use a fishing net or throw a large towel over the hen
  3. Check for leg bands (may indicate ownership)
  4. Provide immediate access to grit if feeding anything but commercial feed
  5. Quarantine for 30 days before introducing to other birds
🥚 Egg-Bound Hen Emergency ▶

Signs of egg-binding (life-threatening):

  1. Straining, squatting frequently, tail pumping
  2. Create a warm bath (95°F/35°C) with Epsom salts (back end only)
  3. Apply vegetable oil around vent area
  4. Keep in warm, dark, quiet space
  5. Seek vet help within 2 hours - this is fatal if untreated
🦊 Predator Attack Aftercare ▶

Caring for attack survivors:

  1. Check for puncture wounds (often hidden under feathers)
  2. Clean wounds with diluted betadine (1:10 ratio with water)
  3. Watch for shock: provide electrolytes and keep at 85°F/29°C
  4. Predator saliva causes infection - antibiotics usually needed
  5. Remove all remaining hens to secure location
